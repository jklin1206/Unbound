# Static-Hold Standards — Isometric Calisthenics Skills

Real-world performance standards for **static / isometric** calisthenics skills,
captured **2026-05-30**. Purpose: ground the UNBOUND skill-rank ladder for the
hold-based skill nodes (levers, planche, handstand, flag, rings, core holds).

**Why this doc exists separately from `strength-standards-harvest.md`:**
[strengthlevel.com](https://strengthlevel.com/) has **no crowd-sourced database
for any of these movements** — they are all timed holds or leverage gates, not
single-set rep maxes. There is no million-lift dataset to lean on. Every number
below is **triangulated from calisthenics coaching sources** (r/bodyweightfitness
Recommended Routine + wiki, GMB, Steven Low / *Overcoming Gravity*, FitnessFAQs
and other coach sites, plus calisthenics world-record data for the ceiling).
Treat them as **community-consensus milestones, not population percentiles.**

## Metric types

- **`seconds`** — a single clean continuous hold at the named position. Form
  standard = the body line a coach would accept (lever parallel to ground, hips
  at shoulder height in the flag, locked/protracted arms in planche, etc.).
- **`leverage gate`** — for the lever / planche families, each progression stage
  (tuck → advanced tuck → straddle → full) is a **separate skill node**. The
  thing being measured is *"can you own this stage at all,"* and ownership =
  holding **that specific leverage** for the seconds in the table. You cannot
  "be Elite at full planche" by holding tuck longer — you change the lever.
  Within a single stage's node, the 5 bands are hold-seconds at that stage.

## Difficulty placement vs. the rep anchors

The brief's elite rep anchors (pull-up 32, muscle-up 17, HSPU 46, dip 44) are
genuinely hard but **acquirable by a dedicated intermediate over a year or two.**
The **full planche** and **full front lever** sit *above* all of them — they are
straight-arm, max-leverage feats that take most athletes **3–5+ years** and that
the majority of even committed calisthenics practitioners never reach. The
**iron cross** is in the same "rare even among advanced" tier. This is why their
bands below top out at modest second-counts: a 30 s full planche is essentially a
**world-class** performance (the standing planche hold WR is ~15 s; the front
lever hold WR is ~57–69 s).

## 1. Core holds

| Skill | metric | Beginner | Novice | Intermediate | Advanced | Elite | conf | sources |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hollow body hold | seconds | 10 | 20 | 30 | 45 | 60+ | **H** | GMB, r/bwf RR, gymnastics core consensus |
| Plank (front) | seconds | 20 | 45 | 60 | 120 | 180+ | **H** | McGill torso-endurance, multiple PT/plank-test sources |
| Side plank | seconds | 15 | 30 | 45 | 60 | 90+ | **M** | PT/plank standards (per side); less hard data than front plank |
| L-sit (floor/parallettes) | seconds | <1 (tuck only) | 5 | 15 | 30 | 45+ | **H** | r/bwf RR, GMB, Antranik, odin.fitness |
| V-sit | leverage gate / seconds | can't | hold 2–3s | 5 | 10 | 20+ | **M** | bwf arena, summerfunfitness; few hard numbers, high compression demand |
| Dragon flag | reps OR seconds hold | bent-knee neg | 1 clean rep / 3s | 5s hold or 5 reps | 10–15s hold / 10 reps | 20s+ hold | **M** | summerfunfitness, bwf arena, T-Nation; coaches mix reps & holds |

**Notes:**
- **Hollow body** and **plank** are the best-grounded numbers in this doc — they
  appear consistently across many independent sources, and the plank has actual
  clinical (McGill) anchoring at the ~2-min "strong" ceiling.
- **L-sit** "Beginner = tuck only" reflects that a true straight-leg L-sit is
  itself the achievement; sub-beginners hold tuck/one-leg variants. 30 s is the
  widely-cited "you own it" milestone (placed at Advanced), 45 s+ is rare.
- **V-sit** is leverage-gated by hamstring/compression more than by raw strength;
  numbers are the softest in the core block.
- **Dragon flag** is genuinely measured both ways in the wild. If UNBOUND wants
  one metric, **seconds of full-extension hold** is the cleaner gate (5/10/20 s).

## 2. Front lever (each stage = separate node)

| Skill | metric | Beginner | Novice | Intermediate | Advanced | Elite | conf | sources |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| FL — tuck | seconds at tuck | can't | 5 | 10 | 20 | 30+ | **M-H** | r/bwf, Movement Athlete, heavyweightcali |
| FL — advanced tuck | seconds at adv. tuck | can't | 3 | 8 | 15 | 25+ | **M** | same; adv-tuck = legs extended, knees over hips |
| FL — straddle | seconds at straddle | can't | 2 | 5 | 15 | 30+ | **M** | coaches: 30–60s straddle before attempting full |
| FL — full | seconds at full | can't | hold 1–2s | 5 | 15 | 30+ | **M** | full-FL hold WR ~57–69s; 30s+ ≈ elite |

**Ownership gate:** counting a stage as "achieved" = a **~5 s clean hold** at that
leverage (the common coaching bar for "you have it"). Full front lever 30 s+ is
near-elite; the world record clean hold is ~57–69 s.

## 3. Back lever (each stage = separate node)

| Skill | metric | Beginner | Novice | Intermediate | Advanced | Elite | conf | sources |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| BL — tuck | seconds at tuck | can't | 5 | 10–15 | 20 | 30+ | **M-H** | calisthenics-hub, Movement Athlete, summerfunfitness |
| BL — advanced tuck | seconds at adv. tuck | can't | 3 | 10 | 15 | 25+ | **M** | same |
| BL — straddle | seconds at straddle | can't | 2 | 5 | 12 | 20+ | **M** | same |
| BL — full | seconds at full | can't | hold 2–3s | 5 | 12 | 20+ | **M** | clean 5s full ≈ "achieved"; back lever is the *easiest* of the three lever-family fulls |

**Note:** Back lever is meaningfully **easier than front lever**, which is easier
than planche. A clean 5 s full back lever is reachable in ~3–6 months by a trained
athlete — so this whole family sits lower in difficulty than the planche family.

## 4. Planche (each stage = separate node — the hardest family)

| Skill | metric | Beginner | Novice | Intermediate | Advanced | Elite | conf | sources |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Planche — tuck | seconds at tuck | can't | 5 | 10 | 20 | 30+ | **M** | GMB, calisthenics.com, OG-followers |
| Planche — advanced tuck | seconds at adv. tuck | can't | 2–3 | 5 | 10 | 20+ | **M** | notoriously hard jump; OG-followers report 8+ sets of 2–3s early |
| Planche — straddle | seconds at straddle | can't | hold 1–2s | 5 | 10 | 20+ | **M** | move up at 10s clean straddle |
| Planche — full | seconds at full | can't | hold 1–2s | 3 | 8 | 15+ | **M-L** | full-planche hold WR ~15s; 8s+ ≈ elite, 15s ≈ world-class |

**Ownership gate:** ~5 s clean hold at a stage = "you own that stage." The
**advanced-tuck → straddle** jump is the famously brutal one. Full planche is the
**single hardest skill in this doc**; an 8 s hold is elite-tier and 15 s approaches
the recorded ceiling. Place full planche *above* every rep anchor in the brief.

## 5. Handstand

| Skill | metric | Beginner | Novice | Intermediate | Advanced | Elite | conf | sources |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Wall handstand (chest-to-wall) | seconds | 10 | 30 | 60 | 90 | 120+ | **H** | r/bwf RR, GMB, odin.fitness — 60s is the canonical "ready to leave the wall" bar |
| Freestanding handstand | seconds | <1 (kicks up) | 5 | 15 | 30 | 60+ | **H** | GMB, Handstand Factory, calisthenics assoc. |

**Notes:**
- Wall handstand is well-grounded: **60 s chest-to-wall** is near-universally
  cited as the prerequisite milestone before freestanding work.
- Freestanding: a repeatable **10–15 s** hold = "officially learned it"
  (Intermediate). **60 s freestanding** is a genuine Elite hand-balance benchmark
  (and the launch point for one-arm work).

## 6. Human flag

| Skill | metric | Beginner | Novice | Intermediate | Advanced | Elite | conf | sources |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Human flag (horizontal) | seconds (per side) | can't (vert/tuck only) | 2–3 | 5 | 10 | 15+ | **M** | calisthenics.com, BERG, calisthenics-corner, calisthenics-family |

**Ownership gate:** a clean **horizontal** hold (body parallel to ground, top
arm pressing, bottom arm pulling) at ~3–5 s = "achieved." Sub-beginners hold the
**vertical/tuck flag** (legs up the pole) which is a different, easier node. 15 s+
horizontal is advanced/elite. Numbers are coach-consensus, low hard data.

## 7. Rings

| Skill | metric | Beginner | Novice | Intermediate | Advanced | Elite | conf | sources |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ring support hold (top, turned out) | seconds | 5 | 15 | 30 | 45 | 60+ | **M-H** | r/bwf RR, Steven Low — 30s turned-out support is the standard gate, 60s solid |
| Iron cross | leverage gate / seconds | can't | hold 1–2s | 3 | 8 | 15+ | **L** | NASM/gymnastics: 2s = competition recognition; Steven Low gives *no* second-standards |

**Notes:**
- **Ring support hold:** the meaningful gate is a **turned-out (RTO) support**;
  30 s turned-out is the commonly cited "you have a real support" bar, used as a
  prerequisite for nearly everything else on rings.
- **Iron cross** is the **lowest-confidence row in the doc.** It's primarily a
  **leverage gate** — you can hold it or you can't, and connective-tissue readiness
  (OG Level 7–8) gates it harder than time does. Gymnastics only requires a **2 s
  hold** for a credited cross. Steven Low's iron-cross article gives progression
  exercises but **explicitly no timed standards.** The 8 s / 15 s "advanced/elite"
  numbers are my **best estimate**, not sourced figures — flag accordingly.

## Confidence summary — what's solid vs. estimated

**Well-sourced (H / M-H)** — multiple independent coaching sources converge,
and in two cases there's clinical or record-book anchoring:
- **Hollow body, front plank, wall handstand, freestanding handstand** — the
  strongest rows. 60 s wall-handstand, 60 s hollow, and the ~2-min "strong" plank
  ceiling are near-universal.
- **L-sit** (30 s = the canonical milestone) and **ring support hold** (30 s RTO).
- **Lever-family *tuck* stages** — tuck FL/BL ~10 s intermediate, ~30 s elite are
  consistently cited.

**Coach-consensus but soft (M)** — directionally trustworthy, exact seconds vary
blog to blog; the *progression order and relative difficulty* are reliable even
where the absolute numbers wobble:
- All **advanced-tuck / straddle / full** lever and planche stages. These are
  triangulated and the ceilings are sanity-checked against world records
  (full FL hold WR ~57–69 s; full planche hold WR ~15 s), but few sources publish
  banded standards, so the Novice/Advanced splits are interpolated.
- **Side plank, V-sit, dragon flag, human flag** — real consensus on the
  milestone (~30 s side plank, ~5 s horizontal flag) but thin on full 5-band data.

**Best estimate, low confidence (L)** — treat as placeholders to be tuned:
- **Iron cross seconds.** It's a leverage/tendon gate with a 2 s competition bar
  and no published time-standards (Steven Low gives none). The advanced/elite
  second-counts are my extrapolation from the planche/cross difficulty tier, not
  a sourced number.

**Cross-cutting caveats:**
1. There is **no percentile data** behind any row — these are "milestones a coach
   would name," not "95th-percentile lifter." Don't market them as population stats.
2. For the **leverage-gated** families, the honest design is: each stage is its own
   node, "achieved" ≈ a **~5 s clean hold at that exact leverage**, and the within-
   node band is just hold duration. Holding an *easier* stage longer must not count
   toward a *harder* stage.
3. **Difficulty ordering is the highest-confidence takeaway:** back lever <
   front lever < planche within the lever families; full planche and full front
   lever sit **above** every rep anchor in the brief; iron cross is in that same
   rare-even-among-advanced tier.
