08 · the key idea
Difficulty = placement, not reps

Where a move sits in the tree is its rank.

User A

100 pushups, nothing harder.

pushup = a low-layer move

→ Novice rank. Impressive reps, easy skill.

vs

User B

a planche (barely).

planche = a top-layer move

→ Ascendant rank. Genuinely elite.

Today both could read "Ascendant." New model: B clearly outranks A — correctly. Your rank = the hardest moves you've proven. The good news: the data already exists — each skill carries a difficulty tier 1–8 that maps almost 1:1 onto Novice→Ascendant.

09 · decision
Within-skill progression · council vote

Ranking up = climbing to a harder move.

pushup → diamond → archer → one-arm. Each is a deeper, higher-rank node — that's the real rank-up. Reps on one move just unlock the next.

(a) keep 9 sub-tiers

status quo dressed up — a second rank ladder inside every move. Drift returns.

(b) one unlock threshold ✓

One bar to open the next move. No sub-tiers. Unanimous council pick.

(c) 3-milestone bar

still a competing axis, still 3× authoring. Just (a) with fewer steps.

"The moment the gate has internal tiers, it stops being a gate and becomes a second rank ladder — exactly the redundancy we're killing."

10 · verdict
"What is AP, really?"

A work score that should retire to one job.

AP fans into the heatmap, the hex, and LV — so as a display currency it's redundant. But as a per-movement cumulative-work rank-gate ledger, it's the one thing nothing else replaces.

keep: rawAP (internal) keep: per-move AP bank unify: visible level → LV retire: gains · provenTier · dead XP
